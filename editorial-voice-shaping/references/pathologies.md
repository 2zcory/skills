# Copy Pathologies

Use these labels when diagnosing why public-facing copy feels stiff or AI-like.

## Common pathologies

- `abstract noun stacking`
  - Too many words like `system`, `structure`, `signal`, `identity`, `surface`, `frame`, or `hierarchy` packed into one sentence.
- `self-explaining copy`
  - The route explains why it exists or how it was designed instead of helping the visitor use it.
- `mirrored rhythm`
  - Several sentences have the same length, cadence, or contrast pattern, which makes the prose feel generated.
- `internal reasoning leak`
  - Product or editorial logic that belongs in design notes appears in public-facing text.
- `manifesto drift`
  - The page starts sounding like a thesis about itself instead of a usable surface.
- `policy memo tone`
  - Constraint-heavy language becomes procedural or defensive instead of clear and honest.
- `curated showcase tone`
  - The copy sounds too arranged, too impressive, or too aware of its own presentation.

## Diagnostic questions

- Is the sentence helping the visitor decide, or helping the author justify the page?
- Would a first-time visitor understand the page faster if this line disappeared?
- Is the wording more conceptual than the route actually needs?
- Is the page sounding defensive because it is naming constraints too often?
- Is the copy preserving a real voice, or only preserving carefulness?
