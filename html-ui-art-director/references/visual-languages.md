# Visual Languages

Choose one primary visual language. Do not blend two unless the task explicitly needs a hybrid and the dominance is still clear.

## Editorial Product

Use when the product should feel authored, intelligent, and high-trust.

- Typography: strong contrast between display and body
- Layout: asymmetry is allowed
- Radius: low to medium
- Shadows: light and sparse
- Background: textured solids, restrained gradients, or paper-like contrast
- Icons: minimal line icons or no icons
- Imagery: large, intentional, cropped with confidence

## Product Premium

Use when the product should feel polished, confident, and conversion-ready.

- Typography: modern, controlled, high legibility
- Layout: dominant hero plus calm support surfaces
- Radius: medium only where it improves touch and friendliness
- Shadows: soft, purposeful, never muddy
- Background: layered neutrals with one accent family
- Icons: consistent outline or duotone set
- Imagery: selective, trust-building, often product-led

## Dense Tooling

Use when the product is powerful, operator-heavy, or workflow-dense.

- Typography: compact, disciplined, low drama
- Layout: rails, split panes, sticky context, tool regions
- Radius: low
- Shadows: minimal; structure should come from spacing and dividers
- Background: controlled tonal steps
- Icons: compact, frequent, highly systematic
- Imagery: usually omitted unless it carries task meaning

## Calm Enterprise

Use when clarity, trust, and low cognitive volatility matter more than flair.

- Typography: conservative, strong rhythm
- Layout: stable grids, clear sections, obvious navigation
- Radius: low to medium
- Shadows: very restrained
- Background: quiet neutrals
- Icons: sparse and familiar
- Imagery: only if it adds trust or explanation

## Bold Consumer

Use when the product should feel lively, expressive, and momentum-driven.

- Typography: more expressive display choices
- Layout: stronger contrast between hero and support
- Radius: medium to high only if it fits the brand voice
- Shadows: punchier but controlled
- Background: richer color and shape composition
- Icons: expressive but consistent
- Imagery: more welcome, but still purposeful

## AI Workspace

Use when the product should feel like a capable creative or analytical environment rather than a brochure.

- Typography: sharp, modern, utilitarian with one expressive accent
- Layout: command surfaces plus canvas or conversation regions
- Radius: low to medium
- Shadows: layered to separate work zones, not to decorate
- Background: atmospheric but muted
- Icons: functional, system-like
- Imagery: abstract support visuals, pattern fields, or product shots

## Selection rule

Choose the language that best matches:

- decision speed
- trust requirement
- emotional tone
- product density
- brand ambition

If the screen starts looking like a template marketplace demo, the visual language is too weak or too blended.
